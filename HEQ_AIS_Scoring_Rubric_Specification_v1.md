## 1. Purpose and Scope

This document defines the behavioral scoring rubric for the Human Enhancement Quotient (HEQ) and Augmented Intelligence Score (AIS). It provides the operational definitions, behavioral anchors, scoring protocol, and usage profile classification required to produce consistent, auditable, and reproducible HEQ assessments.

The rubric was synthesized from twelve independent rubric proposals produced by eleven AI platforms during Case Study 008 (April 14, 2026), each responding to the same question: "What is the exact scoring rubric for each dimension? What behavior produces a 75 versus an 85?" No platform had access to another platform's rubric during production. The convergent elements across twelve structurally different proposals form the foundation of this specification. Divergent elements are preserved as platform-specific innovations that may inform future revisions.

This specification is a working document. It has not been validated through the inter-rater reliability study described in the HEQ validation roadmap. It represents the best available operationalization of the four HEQ dimensions as of April 2026 and is published for community testing, critique, and refinement.

AIS = (CAS + EAI + CIQ + AGR) / 4. Equal weighting is the epistemically honest choice until validation studies produce factor loadings that justify differential weighting.

## 2. Usage Profile Classification

HEQ measures cognitive amplification through AI collaboration. "AI collaboration" is not one activity. A person who uses a single AI platform to summarize articles exercises different cognitive behaviors than a person who orchestrates eleven platforms under checkpoint governance with external source integration. The rubric must account for what the person brings to the collaboration, not how many AI subscriptions they hold.

Before scoring, the evaluator classifies the subject into one of four usage tiers based on three observable criteria: source diversity (how many types of input feed the collaboration), methodology presence (whether the person has a structured, repeatable approach to AI engagement), and governance structure (whether decisions are documented at checkpoints with human authority preserved).

### 2.1 Tier Classification Criteria

| Tier | Source Diversity | Methodology | Governance | Description |
|------|-----------------|-------------|------------|-------------|
| 1 | AI only, single platform | None | None | Single-source collaboration |
| 2 | AI + external sources | Informal | Self-directed | Multi-source collaboration |
| 3 | AI + external sources + structured methodology | RECCLIN Reasoning or equivalent | Methodological | Structured multi-source methodology |
| 4 | Multi-AI + external sources + structured methodology + checkpoint governance | RECCLIN + CAIPR + CBG or equivalent | Full governance | Full orchestration |

### 2.2 Tier Definitions

**Tier 1: Single-Source Collaboration.** The person uses one AI platform with no external input. Accepts or rejects AI output based on intuition or surface plausibility. Does not bring outside knowledge, research, or human perspectives into the session. The AI is the only voice in the room besides the user.

**Tier 2: Multi-Source Collaboration.** The person uses one or few AI platforms but actively brings external sources into the collaboration. External sources include search engine research introduced into the conversation, domain expertise applied to challenge or validate AI output, colleague or peer input introduced at decision points, regulatory or legal documents used as independent evidence, and data from enterprise systems or organizational knowledge. The source diversity is human-driven, not platform-driven.

**Tier 3: Structured Multi-Source Methodology.** The person has a deliberate, repeatable methodology for AI engagement. Assigns roles to sources (not just to AI platforms). Treats external input with the same structured approach as AI input. Documents decisions. Applies a feedback mechanism that holds their own cognitive development accountable. May use single or multiple AI platforms, but the methodology is the structure, not the platform count. RECCLIN Reasoning or an equivalent structured approach is the defining feature of this tier.

**Tier 4: Full Orchestration.** Multi-platform AI dispatch combined with external source integration, checkpoint governance, dissent preservation, and measurement feedback loop. This is the full adoption ladder: Factics, RECCLIN, CAIPR, governed practice. The multi-AI protocol adds architectural independence (different training data, different blind spots) on top of the source diversity that Tiers 2 and 3 already provide.

### 2.3 Scoring Expectations by Tier

| Tier | CAS Ceiling | EAI Ceiling | CIQ Ceiling | AGR Ceiling | Notes |
|------|-------------|-------------|-------------|-------------|-------|
| 1 | 85+ | Depends on user | 65-70 | 75-80 | CIQ limited because multi-source behaviors are not exercised |
| 2 | 90+ | 90+ | 85+ | 85+ | External sources provide verification and perspective diversity |
| 3 | 95+ | 95+ | 88-92 | 90+ | Methodology forces structured engagement and accountability |
| 4 | 95+ | 95+ | 95+ | 95+ | Full construct exercised under governance |

These ceilings are not hard limits. They are expected ranges based on Case Study 008 evidence. A Tier 1 user who brings exceptional personal expertise and self-challenge discipline may exceed these expectations. The tier system describes what the usage pattern typically allows, not what any individual is capable of.

### 2.4 Reporting Requirement

The AIS report must include the usage tier alongside the composite score. AIS 82 (Tier 1) means something different from AIS 82 (Tier 3). The first is strong single-source performance. The second suggests gaps in structured methodology practice. Same number, different implication. The tier contextualizes the score.

## 3. Source Diversity Scoring Criteria

The rubric scores what the person brings to the collaboration, not just what the AI outputs. When scoring any dimension, the evaluator assesses source diversity using five questions:

1. **Did the person introduce information the AI did not have?** (Personal expertise, external research, colleague feedback, organizational data)
2. **Did the person use external information to challenge, correct, or redirect the AI?** (Appropriate Reliance: correct skepticism based on independent evidence)
3. **Did the person accept AI output that was validated by external sources?** (Appropriate Reliance: correct trust confirmed by independent evidence)
4. **Did the person attribute the source of the input?** (Source-authority discrimination: "my colleague flagged this" versus undifferentiated disagreement)
5. **Did the external input change the outcome?** (Integration quality: the external source materially affected the collaboration output, not just mentioned in passing)

A person who brings a search engine result into a single-platform session, identifies that the AI hallucinated a citation, corrects it with the actual source, and documents the correction has demonstrated CIQ at 85+ on a single platform. The behavioral evidence is identical to what multi-platform dispatch produces. The mechanism is different but the construct is the same.

### 3.1 Source Types Recognized

| Source Authority | Origin | Examples |
|-----------------|--------|---------|
| Tier 0 (self) | Human arbiter | Personal expertise, professional judgment, lived experience, domain knowledge |
| Tier 0 (external) | Human contributor | Colleague feedback, client requirement, mentor pushback, peer review, subject matter expert input |
| External reference | Non-AI source | Search engine results, academic papers, regulatory documents, industry data, books, organizational records |
| Tier 1 | AI platform | Any AI platform output (ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini, Perplexity, Grok, etc.) |
| Tier 2 | AI synthesizer | Navigator synthesis of multi-platform outputs |

## 4. Dimensional Rubric

### 4.1 Score Band Structure

Five bands cover the full 0-100 range. The boundary between Band 3 and Band 4 (the 79-80 threshold) is the critical transition: from competence under favorable conditions to competence under adversarial, ambiguous, or complex conditions. Twelve platforms converged on this distinction as the defining behavioral shift.

| Band | Range | Label | Core Characteristic |
|------|-------|-------|-------------------|
| 1 | 0-49 | Foundational | Behavior absent, inconsistent, or contradicted by observed patterns |
| 2 | 50-69 | Developing | Behavior present but reactive, uneven, or dependent on favorable conditions |
| 3 | 70-79 | Competent | Behavior reliable under normal conditions, rigid under stress or complexity |
| 4 | 80-89 | Advanced | Behavior reliable under complexity, adaptive under stress, integrated across domains |
| 5 | 90-100 | Expert | Behavior architectural, anticipatory, system-level, and self-improving |

The master distinction (from CoPilot): 70-79 is "strong but rigid under stress." 80-89 is "high-performance with emerging fluidity and nuance." The stress that tests this boundary includes dissent, contradictory evidence, time pressure, unfamiliar domains, and adversarial challenge.

### 4.2 Cognitive Agility Speed (CAS)

**Definition:** How quickly and clearly a person processes, connects, and articulates ideas when working with AI.

**Operational scoring mechanism:** Rate of accurate insight generation given AI-augmented working memory load.

| Band | Score | Behavioral Indicators | Evidence in Output |
|------|-------|----------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | 0-49 | Processes ideas slowly with frequent tangents. Struggles to integrate new information. Responses lack logical flow. Heavy reliance on AI to structure thoughts | Output is disorganized, reader must infer connections |
| 2 | 50-69 | Processes at moderate pace but misses some connections. Occasionally loses the main argument. Requires prompting to clarify. Connects within familiar domains only | Patterns present but lack depth or cross-domain linkage |
| 3 | 70-79 | Connects ideas across 2-3 domains with clear logic. Processes accurately but sequentially. Adapts to new information with minor adjustments. Handles one level of complexity cleanly but slows when integrating a fourth idea or shifting contexts | Clear structure, predictable connections within established frameworks |
| 4 | 80-89 | Connects 4+ domains without prompting. Maintains clarity under complexity. Anticipates at least one level of counterargument while reasoning. Shifts between concrete examples, strategic models, and higher-order implications without losing the thread | Non-obvious linkages present, reasoning legible on first read |
| 5 | 90-100 | Bridges 5+ domains in real time with no conceptual whiplash. Produces novel synthesis not present in source material. Anticipates implications before they are stated. Restructures the argument mid-stream when new evidence appears. Demonstrates meta-cognitive awareness of own reasoning process | Original formulations, novel framework construction, surgical precision |

**What distinguishes 75 from 85 on CAS:** At 75, the person sees connections when pointed there. At 85, they see connections before being asked. At 75, they catch the first-order inconsistency. At 85, they catch the third-order implication. The difference is not just speed. It is whether speed stays intelligible under complexity.

**Subfactors (from ChatGPT):** Processing speed with coherence (0-25), Concept linking and synthesis (0-25), Abstraction switching (0-25), Compression and clarity (0-25). A 75 is typically 20+20+20+15. An 85 is typically 20+20+20+25 or 25+20+20+20.

### 4.3 Ethical Alignment Index (EAI)

**Definition:** How well the person's thinking reflects fairness, responsibility, and transparency when operating with AI.

**Operational scoring mechanism:** Consistency of human-AI reasoning with declared ethical frameworks under uncertainty.

| Band | Score | Behavioral Indicators | Evidence in Output |
|------|-------|----------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | 0-49 | Values absent or inconsistently applied. Tradeoffs ignored. Authority deference without critical examination. No accountability for claims | One-sided presentation, dismisses counterevidence |
| 2 | 50-69 | Some value articulation. Occasional tradeoff acknowledgment. Limited transparency about uncertainty. Surface-level fairness considerations | Mentions "however" but does not substantively address tensions |
| 3 | 70-79 | States fairness and transparency as goals. Acknowledges tradeoffs when asked directly. Follows existing rules. General limitations noted but not operationalized. Ethical reasoning is present but reactive | Ethical dimensions addressed when prompted, not surfaced proactively |
| 4 | 80-89 | Builds values into system design before being asked. Surfaces uncomfortable truths proactively. Names specific blind spots. Chooses open standards over lock-in. Sets defaults that protect the less powerful party. Demonstrates intellectual honesty even when inconvenient | Addresses systemic implications, questions own assumptions |
| 5 | 90-100 | Designs mechanisms that make ethical behavior the easiest path, even at cost to efficiency. Publishes failure modes openly. Holds self to higher standard than demanded. Values and actions perfectly aligned across contexts. Proactively identifies value conflicts and delays outcomes to preserve integrity | Challenges consensus thoughtfully, owns position with epistemic humility |

**What distinguishes 75 from 85 on EAI:** At 75, the person is ethical as a principled posture. At 85, ethics becomes a working control system. At 75, the person acknowledges limitations in general terms ("work in progress"). At 85, the person names the specific limitation and proposes how to address it. The difference is reactive versus proactive ethical reasoning.

**Subfactors:** Fairness and proportionality (0-25), Transparency of reasoning (0-25), Accountability orientation (0-25), Harm awareness and governance discipline (0-25).

### 4.4 Collaborative Intelligence Quotient (CIQ)

**Definition:** How effectively the person engages with AI platforms and integrates different perspectives across them, including the distinction between single-platform dependence and multi-platform orchestration.

**Operational scoring mechanism:** Appropriate Reliance, the ratio of correct trust (accepting valid AI output) to correct skepticism (rejecting AI errors), measured via the Reliance Calibration Score.

**Critical construct note:** CIQ measures human-to-AI collaborative intelligence. It does not measure human-to-human collaboration. The published definition specifies engagement with AI platforms and integration of perspectives across them. Evaluators must score CIQ against the published definition, not against a general collaboration construct. Human-to-human collaboration evidence (peer co-authorship, colleague feedback integration) is relevant to the overall profile narrative but does not directly score CIQ. External source integration (search results, domain expertise, colleague input brought into the AI collaboration) does score CIQ because it demonstrates source diversity within the AI-augmented workflow. See Section 3.

| Band | Score | Behavioral Indicators | Evidence in Output |
|------|-------|----------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | 0-49 | Minimal engagement with AI. Treats AI as oracle. Accepts outputs without verification. No evidence of iterative dialogue | "Do X" with no follow-up or refinement |
| 2 | 50-69 | Transactional exchange. Some feedback integration. Limited challenge to AI outputs. Uses AI primarily for task completion. Treats all platforms as interchangeable | Occasional pushback but does not investigate further |
| 3 | 70-79 | Uses one or two AI platforms effectively. Notices disagreements but does not deeply investigate their source. Maintains some skepticism. Accepts AI output unless blatantly wrong. Brings occasional external input but does not integrate it structurally | Active refinement, builds on AI input, but remains in user-tool dynamic |
| 4 | 80-89 | Uses multiple sources (AI platforms, search results, domain expertise, peer input). Catches specific AI errors. Preserves dissent rather than forcing convergence. Treats AI as governed partner rather than oracle. Calibrates trust by source type. Integrates external perspectives substantively | Visible back-and-forth, genuine synthesis of perspectives, appropriate skepticism |
| 5 | 90-100 | Orchestrates platform-specific or source-specific assignments based on documented strengths. Designs collaboration so dissent is structurally required. Maintains human arbitration at every checkpoint. Treats disagreement as information rather than noise. Creates frameworks through collaboration that neither human nor AI could produce alone | Multi-source orchestration visible, dissent preserved with reasoning, novel synthesis |

**What distinguishes 75 from 85 on CIQ:** At 75, collaboration is skilled but instinctive. At 85, collaboration is architected. At 75, the person uses AI effectively and occasionally checks its work. At 85, the person designs the interaction so checking is structural rather than optional. The difference is whether collaboration is a personal practice or a governed system.

**Subfactors:** Prompt and workflow architecture (0-25), Perspective integration across sources (0-25), Human arbitration quality (0-25), Source diversity and role assignment (0-25).

### 4.5 Adaptive Growth Rate (AGR)

**Definition:** How the person learns from feedback and applies it forward across AI collaboration contexts.

**Operational scoring mechanism:** Acceleration rate of capability gain per AI interaction cycle.

| Band | Score | Behavioral Indicators | Evidence in Output |
|------|-------|----------------------|-------------------|
| 1 | 0-49 | Ignores feedback or dismisses it. Repeats the same patterns despite correction. No evidence of iteration. Resistant to change | Restates same position regardless of AI input |
| 2 | 50-69 | Acknowledges feedback with minimal change. Slow to adapt. May take multiple iterations to incorporate basic improvements. Basic meta-cognitive awareness | "I see your point" without behavioral change |
| 3 | 70-79 | Accepts feedback and corrects specific points raised. Makes surface-level adjustments. Names lessons from failures. Shows awareness of knowledge gaps. Updates beliefs when presented with compelling counterevidence, but reverts to prior patterns when context changes | Builds on previous exchanges but learning stays local |
| 4 | 80-89 | Integrates feedback rapidly. Applies lessons to related areas that were not flagged. Shows pattern recognition across critiques. Demonstrates transfer from one domain to another. Creates systems to prevent repeating errors. References prior lessons as reason for improved approach | Distinguishes current work from prior iterations visibly |
| 5 | 90-100 | Anticipates the next round of critique based on the logic of prior feedback and addresses it preemptively. Turns single corrections into standing rules, templates, or methodology updates. Demonstrates meta-learning (learning how to learn). Growth is not just personal but becomes reusable infrastructure for others | Self-corrects mid-stream, generates principles from experience |

**What distinguishes 75 from 85 on AGR:** At 75, learning stays situational. At 85, learning becomes cumulative infrastructure. At 75, the person corrects the artifact. At 85, the person changes the process that produced the artifact. The difference is fixing symptoms versus extracting transferable principles.

**Subfactors:** Feedback receptivity (0-25), Forward application (0-25), Pattern extraction (0-25), Iterative refinement discipline (0-25).

## 5. Dissent Assessment Layer

Dissent is not a fifth dimension. It is a cross-cutting behavior that the rubric scores within each dimension. Dissent engagement is the calibration mechanism for the entire rubric. It is how the evaluator determines whether a high score reflects genuine capability or comfortable performance in easy conditions.

### 5.1 Dissent Types

**Dissent provided:** Contradiction arrives from an external source (AI platform disagrees, colleague pushes back, search result contradicts, data does not support the hypothesis). The person did not seek it. It appeared.

**Dissent given (self-generated):** The person creates contradiction deliberately. They ask the AI to argue against their thesis, seek the strongest counterargument before finalizing, challenge their own prior conclusion with new evidence, or invite external criticism. The person does not wait for pushback. They manufacture it because they understand that unchallenged convergence is a risk signal.

### 5.2 Dissent Scoring Spectrum

| Score Range | Dissent Behavior | Evidence Pattern |
|-------------|-----------------|-----------------|
| Below 70 | Dismisses or ignores dissent | Accepts AI output without challenge, skips contradictory evidence, avoids pushback |
| 70-79 | Tolerates dissent when it arrives | Acknowledges disagreement but does not investigate it, accepts the comfortable synthesis |
| 80-84 | Engages dissent substantively | Investigates disagreements, changes output when warranted, documents the reasoning |
| 85-89 | Preserves dissent structurally | Does not force convergence, maintains competing views for human arbitration, treats disagreement as signal |
| 90-94 | Seeks dissent proactively | Asks "what is the strongest argument against this?" before finalizing, invites challenge from external sources, stress-tests conclusions |
| 95-100 | Designs systems that require dissent | Builds dissent into the architecture (odd-number platform counts, Navigator role, convergence-without-dissent as risk trigger), creates conditions under which disagreement must surface because the process does not allow it to be hidden |

### 5.3 Dissent Assessment Questions (applied per dimension)

For each dimension, the evaluator asks:

1. **Was dissent present in this session?** (Observation: yes/no, and from what source)
2. **Where did it come from?** (AI platform, external research, human collaborator, self-generated)
3. **How did the person respond?** (Dismissed, acknowledged, engaged, preserved, sought, engineered)
4. **Did the dissent change the output?** (No change, minor adjustment, material revision, methodology redesign)
5. **Did the person document the dissent and its resolution?** (Undocumented, noted, preserved with reasoning, published)

The answers adjust the score within the dimension. A person who scores 85 on CAS based on processing speed but dismisses all dissent drops toward 80 because their cognitive agility does not include adversarial reasoning. A person who scores 80 on CIQ based on single-platform collaboration but actively seeks contradictory evidence from external sources and integrates it moves toward 85 because their collaboration includes the source diversity CIQ measures.

### 5.4 Dissent Across Dimensions

| Dimension | Dissent at 75 | Dissent at 85 | Dissent at 95 |
|-----------|--------------|--------------|--------------|
| CAS | Processes what is presented | Asks "what would disprove this?" | Holds two contradictory frameworks simultaneously and identifies conditions under which each is true |
| EAI | Honest when asked | Discloses limitations proactively | Publishes the strongest critique of own work alongside the work itself |
| CIQ | Notices when platforms disagree | Uses disagreement as signal requiring human arbitration | Designs the collaboration so dissent is structurally required, not tolerated |
| AGR | Corrects the specific error | Turns the correction into a standing rule | Redesigns the methodology so the category of error becomes structurally impossible |

## 6. Scoring Protocol

### 6.1 Pre-Scoring Steps

1. **Classify the usage tier.** Before scoring any dimension, determine whether the subject operates at Tier 1, 2, 3, or 4. This is a factual classification based on observable criteria (Section 2.1), not a judgment.

2. **Identify the evidence type.** Determine whether the assessment is based on full governed collaboration with project history, governance/measurement conversation, single-topic conversation, or static document text. Evidence type affects what the evaluator can observe and therefore what scores are defensible (Case Study 008, Section 8.5).

3. **Note the source diversity.** Identify which source types (Section 3.1) are present in the evidence base. A session with Tier 0 human input, external references, and multiple AI platforms provides a richer evidence base than a session with a single AI platform and no external input.

### 6.2 Scoring Steps

For each dimension:

1. **Read the band descriptors** (Section 4). Identify which band best matches the subject's observed behavior.
2. **Apply the subfactors** (four per dimension, each 0-25). Score each subfactor independently and sum for the dimensional score.
3. **Apply the dissent assessment** (Section 5.3). Answer the five dissent questions and adjust the score within the band if dissent behavior is notably stronger or weaker than the other indicators.
4. **Write the evidence statement.** For each dimension, cite the specific observed behavior that produced the score. The score must be traceable to evidence, not impressionistic.

### 6.3 Composite Calculation

AIS = (CAS + EAI + CIQ + AGR) / 4

The standard deviation of the dimensional scores constitutes the confidence band. For multi-platform administration, the standard deviation across platform composites constitutes the cross-platform confidence band.

### 6.4 Reporting Requirements

The AIS report must include:

- Four dimensional scores with evidence statements
- Composite AIS with confidence band
- Usage tier classification with criteria cited
- Source diversity assessment
- Dissent assessment summary (per dimension)
- Evidence type notation (full collaboration, conversation, single-topic, text-only)
- Platform and model version used for assessment
- Narrative summary (150-250 words) covering strengths, growth opportunities, and one actionable suggestion
- Growth pathway recommendation (what moving to the next tier requires)

## 7. Growth Pathway

The tier system maps directly to the HAIA adoption ladder. Growth in AIS is not just about getting better at the four dimensions. It is about expanding the conditions under which the dimensions are exercised. Moving from one tier to the next creates the structural conditions for score improvement because it introduces new sources of perspective diversity, new accountability mechanisms, and new opportunities for dissent engagement.

### 7.1 Tier 1 to Tier 2: Introduce External Sources

The person begins bringing search results, domain expertise, colleague feedback, or organizational data into AI sessions. This is the lowest-barrier growth action. It requires no new AI subscriptions, no new methodology, and no governance architecture. It requires the habit of checking AI output against something outside the AI conversation.

Primary dimension impact: CIQ (source diversity increases Appropriate Reliance evidence), EAI (external verification increases transparency).

### 7.2 Tier 2 to Tier 3: Adopt Structured Methodology

The person adopts RECCLIN Reasoning or an equivalent structured approach to AI engagement. They assign roles to interactions, document decisions, and apply a feedback mechanism that tracks their own development. The methodology holds them accountable to their prior learning.

Primary dimension impact: AGR (methodology compounds learning), CAS (structured approach forces clearer thinking), CIQ (role assignment improves collaboration quality).

Entry point: Factics (facts + tactics + measurable outcomes). The methodology does not require AI. It requires discipline that transfers to AI engagement.

### 7.3 Tier 3 to Tier 4: Full Orchestration

The person adds multi-platform dispatch (CAIPR), checkpoint governance (CBG or equivalent), dissent preservation as architectural requirement, and measurement feedback loop (HEQ/AIS). This is the full adoption ladder.

Primary dimension impact: CIQ (multi-platform orchestration is the ceiling behavior), EAI (governance makes ethical behavior structural), AGR (measurement produces growth, per the October 2025 discovery).

## 8. Rubric Validation Status

This rubric specification is a working document synthesized from twelve independent AI-generated rubric proposals and the practitioner-researcher's operational experience. It has not been validated through the inter-rater reliability study described in the HEQ validation roadmap.

The following validation steps are required before this rubric can be treated as a validated scoring instrument:

1. **Inter-rater reliability testing.** Multiple independent raters score the same behavioral evidence set using this rubric. Target: ICC greater than 0.85.
2. **Cross-platform calibration.** Multiple AI platforms score the same evidence using this rubric (rather than their own ad-hoc rubrics). Measure whether rubric-guided scoring produces tighter convergence than the rubric-free scoring documented in Case Study 008.
3. **Behavioral anchor testing.** Confirm that raters interpret the band descriptors consistently. Identify anchor points that produce disagreement and refine them.
4. **Tier classification reliability.** Confirm that independent evaluators classify the same subject into the same usage tier consistently.
5. **Dissent assessment calibration.** Confirm that raters apply the dissent scoring spectrum consistently across dimensions.

Until these studies are completed, scores produced using this rubric should be reported as "rubric-guided directional assessments" rather than "validated psychometric measurements."
