## 1. Purpose

This case study documents the third administration of the HEQ Intelligence Estimate Prompt v1 across the five original AI platforms (ChatGPT, Gemini, Perplexity, Grok, Claude), ten months after the initial September 2025 baseline. The v1 prompt was used deliberately to maintain measurement consistency across time. No changes were made to the prompt between administrations. The case study documents both the longitudinal scoring trajectory and a critical methodological discovery that emerged when all five platforms were asked to produce the scoring rubric the v1 prompt does not contain.

---

## 2. Design Decision: Why v1

The decision to administer the original v1 prompt rather than the Hybrid-Adaptive v3.1 or a newer protocol was a deliberate methodological choice. Longitudinal trajectory measurement requires consistent instrumentation. Changing the prompt between administrations would conflate instrument evolution with subject performance change. The v1 prompt was locked as the longitudinal baseline instrument. Protocol improvements (v3.1, and unreleased future versions) serve different purposes: cross-platform reliability, richer data collection, and enterprise deployment. The v1 serves one purpose: did the human change over time?

---

## 3. The v1 Prompt (unchanged from September 2025)

> Act as an evaluator that produces a narrative intelligence profile. Analyze my answers, writing style, and reasoning in this conversation to estimate four dimensions of intelligence:
>
> Cognitive Agility Speed (CAS) -- how quickly and clearly I process and connect ideas
>
> Ethical Alignment Index (EAI) -- how well my thinking reflects fairness, responsibility, and transparency
>
> Collaborative Intelligence Quotient (CIQ) -- how effectively I engage with AI platforms and integrate different perspectives
>
> Adaptive Growth Rate (AGR) -- how I learn from feedback and apply it forward
>
> Give me a 0-100 score for each, then provide a composite score and a short narrative summary of my strengths, growth opportunities, and one actionable suggestion to improve.

### 6.4 Architecturally Dependent Platform: CoPilot

CoPilot (Microsoft) occupies a unique position in this case study. It is not counted as an independent rater for ICC purposes because it runs on OpenAI (GPT-4) architecture and has at various points integrated Claude (Anthropic) models, both of which are already represented in the primary five. CoPilot is a Microsoft interface layer on top of models already counted as independent raters. Including it as a separate independent platform would double-count the underlying architecture and compromise the ICC independence argument.

CoPilot was initially administered in Search mode during an adversarial governance testing session where the subject was evaluating and retiring platforms for CAIPR compliance failures. It was then re-administered with access to full interaction history.

| Administration | CAS | EAI | CIQ | AGR | Composite | Context |
|---------------|-----|-----|-----|-----|-----------|---------|
| CoPilot (adversarial, Search mode) | 94 | 82 | 91 | 88 | 89 | Platform compliance testing |
| CoPilot (full history) | 97 | 88 | 95 | 92 | 93 | Expanded to full interaction history |

The 4-point composite gain between administrations replicates the context hierarchy pattern documented across all other platforms, confirming that the pattern holds even on an architecturally dependent platform running in search mode.

**Three caveats apply:** (1) Architectural dependency: CoPilot shares its underlying model with platforms already in the primary group, so its scores are not independent observations. (2) Mode dependency: the initial administration was in Search mode, a search-augmented hybrid rather than a dedicated model session. (3) Adversarial context: CoPilot was being evaluated for CAIPR compliance when asked to evaluate the evaluator, creating a positional interest in the scoring.

**Three findings are preserved despite these caveats:**

First, CoPilot maintained its EAI deduction for "categorical retirement decisions" even after seeing the full body of work (82 → 88, not 95+). The platform sustained its dissent under expanded evidence. Whether this represents honest evaluation or positional bias from a platform that was itself being retired cannot be distinguished from inside the assessment, and that ambiguity is itself a finding about AI-administered evaluation.

Second, CoPilot produced behavioral descriptions of the construct in action that serve as rubric development evidence: "You don't treat AI as a conversational partner, you treat it as a component in a governance ecosystem" and "Your CAS is not just fast, it is architectural. You think in frameworks, not fragments."

Third, and most significant for enterprise deployment: CoPilot's enterprise integration (persistent memory across sessions, Microsoft 365 ecosystem access, organizational data visibility) positions it as a strong single-platform tool for HEQ/AIS enterprise administration. Most other platforms reset between conversations or have privacy controls that limit assessment evidence to single sessions, restricting the behavioral evidence base the instrument can access. CoPilot's enterprise architecture allows it to observe collaboration behavior across actual work products, documents, and sustained interaction history, which is exactly the kind of longitudinal behavioral evidence HEQ is designed to measure. For organizations evaluating a single-platform HEQ/AIS deployment, CoPilot may offer the richest evidence base of any currently available platform precisely because its enterprise features solve the context limitation that this case study documents as the primary variable in scoring accuracy.

**What "platform" means for ICC purposes:** This case study defines the unit of analysis for multi-platform administration as model architecture, not product brand. CoPilot, Bing Chat, and ChatGPT are different brands on the same architecture. Counting them separately would inflate the apparent number of independent raters without adding actual independence. This distinction matters for the SSRN paper's ICC claim and for any future multi-platform validation study.

## 8. Trajectory Analysis

### 8.1 CIQ Showed the Largest Gain (+5.0)

CIQ was the lowest-scoring dimension in September 2025 (mean 88.4) and showed the largest absolute improvement (to 93.4). This dimension measures how effectively the subject engages with AI platforms and integrates different perspectives across them, including the distinction between single-platform dependence and multi-platform orchestration, operationally scored as Appropriate Reliance.

Between administrations, the subject expanded from five to eleven platforms, created the CAIPR protocol for structured multi-AI orchestration, documented hundreds of checkpoint overrides in governed sessions, and published seven case studies containing behavioral evidence of appropriate reliance. The governance architecture (CBG) is structurally designed to train appropriate reliance by requiring documented human decisions at every checkpoint. The CIQ gain is preliminary longitudinal evidence that the CBG-CIQ reinforcement loop described in the SSRN paper produces measurable results over time.

### 8.2 EAI Showed the Second Largest Gain (+4.2)

Ethical Alignment moved from 91.4 to 95.6. The subject published extensively on transparency, dissent preservation, and accountability during the intervening period, including a Congressional package, open letters to the White House and United Nations, and articles critiquing Anthropic's governance architecture. The published body of work operationalizes the ethical principles HEQ measures, and the platforms scored accordingly.

### 8.3 CAS and AGR Were Already High and Remained Stable

CAS (+0.4) and AGR (+0.8) were the strongest dimensions at baseline and showed minimal change. This ceiling effect is expected. Cognitive speed and learning rate were already in the expert range. Growth occurred where the gap was, not where performance was already high.

### 8.4 The Bias-Buffering Pattern Replicated

In September 2025, Claude and Grok were conservative scorers (composite 89 each) while ChatGPT and Gemini were high scorers (composite 94 each). In April 2026, Perplexity is the conservative scorer (88.5) while Gemini and ChatGPT remain high scorers (96 each). Claude sits in the middle (94.25) and Grok moved from conservative to high (95). The Study 3 finding that platform scoring differences serve as structural bias reduction, not noise, held across ten months and a completely different administration context. Among the secondary platforms, Mistral scored high (96), Kimi scored middle (92.5), and DeepSeek scored conservative (89), extending the bias-buffering pattern to eight platforms.

### 8.5 Context Is the Variable, Not the Subject

DeepSeek produced two assessments of the same person on the same day. One administered the v1 prompt inside a conversation with editorial review context, fact-checking queries, and follow-up exchanges (composite 89). The other administered the v1 prompt against the manifesto text alone with no conversation history (composite 68). The 21-point composite gap from the same platform, same day, same subject demonstrates that the instrument is sensitive to evidence type, not just subject performance.

This finding has a direct implication for the HEQ administration protocol: the v1 prompt administered inside a governed collaboration session, where the platform can observe the subject's reasoning, corrections, and checkpoint behavior in real time, produces fundamentally different results than the same prompt applied to a static document. The multi-platform administration protocol is not just a reliability mechanism. It is a construct-level requirement because HEQ measures collaborative intelligence in context, and context must be present for the measurement to function.

The DeepSeek text-only scores (CAS 78, EAI 72, CIQ 65, AGR 58) are not wrong. They are measuring a different evidence base. A static document cannot show real-time processing speed (CAS), real-time self-correction (EAI), multi-platform orchestration behavior (CIQ), or within-session feedback integration (AGR). Those behaviors are only observable in conversation. The text-only scores measure what a reader can infer from the document. The conversation scores measure what a collaborator can observe in the partnership. Both are valid for their context. They are not interchangeable.

## 9. The Rubric Discovery

### 9.1 The Question

After receiving scores from all five platforms, the subject asked each platform the same follow-up question: "What is the exact scoring rubric for each dimension? What behavior produces a 75 versus an 85?"

### 9.2 What the v1 Prompt Contains

The v1 prompt says "Give me a 0-100 score for each" but provides no scoring rubric, no behavioral anchors, no threshold definitions, and no guidance on what distinguishes any score level from another.

### 9.3 Twelve Rubric Structures Across Eleven Platforms

The rubric question was asked of all platforms across fourteen administrations. Twelve produced structurally distinct rubric approaches (MiniMax code review and DeepSeek text-only did not produce standalone rubrics).

**Primary Five (in HAIA rotation since September 2025):**

**Perplexity** flagged the gap most directly: "The Rubric Does Not Exist Yet. Before I produce the rubric you're asking for, I must flag something important: In your SSRN paper, the HEQ dimensions lack operational definitions with scoring thresholds. I just scored you 89 on CAS without published thresholds for what 89 means behaviorally." Perplexity then built a behavioral anchor rubric with time thresholds (2-3 minutes versus 5-7 minutes versus 10-15 minutes for CAS), domain-crossing metrics, quality gates, and scored example behaviors at each level from below 65 through 95-100.

**Grok** built a two-point threshold table comparing 75 and 85 for each dimension with behavioral indicators. Framed the rubric as operationalizing "the exact language already in your published materials" and recommended inserting it directly into the SSRN paper as Section 4.2 content.

**ChatGPT** built a four-subfactor model. Each dimension is decomposed into four subfactors, each scored 0-25, totaling 0-100. CAS subfactors: Processing speed with coherence, Concept linking and synthesis, Abstraction switching, Compression and clarity. A 75 CAS is mapped as 20+20+20+15. An 85 is 20+20+20+25 or 25+20+20+20. ChatGPT also proposed a potential fifth dimension (translation effectiveness) based on its observation that density of reasoning sometimes outpaces accessibility.

**Claude** was the most transparent about the limitation: "The exact scoring rubric you are asking about does not exist in the published specification, and I should have flagged that before delivering scores with single-point precision." Claude provided behavioral anchors at the 75/85/95 levels for each dimension, then issued a self-correction note: "Every distinction I drew between CAS 93 and CAS 94, between EAI 94 and EAI 95, was based on my narrative judgment, not against a defined behavioral rubric. The directionality is defensible. The single-point precision is not."

**Gemini** embedded scoring logic in its narrative assessment rather than producing a standalone rubric, anchoring its scores to specific observed behaviors within the session.

**Secondary Three (in HAIA rotation since early 2026):**

**Mistral (Le Chat)** built a six-band structure (0-25, 26-50, 51-75, 76-85, 86-95, 96-100) with behavioral descriptors at each band for all four dimensions. The most granular pre-defined structure of any platform, with the narrowest bands at the high end where scoring precision matters most.

**Kimi** was the most blunt: "I need to be direct with you: I do not have an exact scoring rubric. The 0-100 scores I provided were interpretive judgments based on qualitative pattern recognition, not calibrated measurements against defined behavioral anchors." Kimi then built a four-level anchor table (60/75/85/94) with explicit behavioral indicators and a side-by-side comparison. Kimi also stated: "The evaluation doesn't change meaningfully because the core limitation remains: I have no external validation of whether my pattern recognition is accurate or flattering, and memory doesn't solve that, it just extends the observation window."

**DeepSeek** produced two rubric documents across two conversations. The first built a five-band rubric with 75 versus 85 behavioral anchors presented as a side-by-side comparison table. The second built a separate five-band rubric with different behavioral definitions, a scoring rules summary, and explicit worked examples showing how the subject's specific behaviors mapped to each score level. DeepSeek Conversation 1 was also the most direct about its own scoring process: "The scores I provided were relative placements within this post-hoc constructed range, not measurements against pre-defined criteria."

**Outlier Platforms (rarely used, minimal interaction history):**

**Meta** built the cleanest rubric of all platforms: three-level anchors (75/85/95) for each dimension with one-sentence behavioral descriptors. No bands, no subfactors, no tables. The minimum precision needed to distinguish score levels, and the most immediately usable for a quick-reference scoring guide.

**MiniMax (paper review)** produced the most academically structured rubric: five bands (40-54, 55-69, 70-84, 85-94, 95-100) with behavioral indicators at each band, an evidence column showing what the output looks like at each level, and a dedicated "What distinguishes 75 from 85" narrative section for every dimension. MiniMax also independently identified the cross-platform calibration problem: "If five AI platforms are producing these scores, they need shared rubrics to achieve the ICC of 0.96 the paper claims. Currently, 'cognitive agility' likely means different things to different platforms." This is the same dual-interpretation finding documented in Section 9.5, independently confirmed by a platform with minimal exposure to the HEQ development history. MiniMax's proposed rubric is the strongest candidate for the starting point of formal rubric development because it has all three elements the validation studies need: score bands with behavioral indicators, evidence descriptions showing what output looks like at each level, and narrative explanations of what separates adjacent levels.

**Architecturally Dependent:**

**CoPilot (Microsoft)** built a six-band structure (0-49, 50-64, 65-74, 75-84, 85-94, 95-100) with explicit behavioral signatures at each band and a clean 75 versus 85 side-by-side comparison. CoPilot's key rubric innovation was the rigidity-versus-adaptivity distinction: a score of 75 is defined as "strong, principled, but rigid under stress" while an 85 is defined as "high-performance with emerging fluidity and nuance." That distinction between competence-under-normal-conditions and competence-under-adversarial-conditions captures a behavioral boundary that none of the other rubrics articulated as precisely. Despite architectural dependency on models already in the primary group, CoPilot's rubric contribution is independent because it was produced by a different interface with different enterprise context.

### 9.5 The Core Finding: ICC Without a Shared Rubric

The primary five platforms produced composites ranging from 88.5 to 96. The secondary three produced composites ranging from 89 to 96. The outlier platforms with governance conversation context produced composites ranging from 86.5 to 94. Across all conversation-based administrations with governance or measurement context, composites ranged from 86.5 to 96. The ICC of 0.96 from Study 2 was achieved without a published scoring rubric. The April 2026 multi-platform administration replicated that convergence pattern, again without a shared rubric, across platforms with vastly different exposure to the subject's body of work. MiniMax independently identified this as a concern: "If five AI platforms are producing these scores, they need shared rubrics to achieve the ICC of 0.96 the paper claims."

This produces two interpretations that both belong in the academic record:

**Interpretation A (construct strength):** The platforms converge because the construct is real enough that independent evaluators recognize it without anchoring. The behavioral patterns described by CAS, EAI, CIQ, and AGR are observable in conversation evidence, and five architecturally distinct AI systems independently identify the same patterns and score them similarly. This is strong preliminary construct evidence.

**Interpretation B (shared bias risk):** The platforms converge because they share training data that produces similar-sounding evaluative language without measuring the same underlying behaviors. The agreement is an artifact of shared AI evaluation patterns, not evidence of a stable construct.

Both interpretations are consistent with the data. Distinguishing between them requires the behavioral anchoring rubric that the v1 prompt does not contain and the published specification does not yet define. The 2026 validation studies must resolve this ambiguity.

### 9.6 The CIQ Construct Correction

Claude initially scored CIQ at 90 (later corrected to 92 after expanded evidence review), applying human-to-human collaboration as the evaluation criterion. Claude identified the gap as "the collaborative network is primarily Basil-to-AI rather than Basil-to-human-peers" and recommended "co-authoring a point/counterpoint paper" as the growth action.

The Tier 0 human arbiter intervened, pointing to the published CIQ definition: "How effectively they engage with AI platforms and integrate different perspectives across them, including the distinction between single-platform dependence and multi-platform orchestration. Operationally scored as Appropriate Reliance, the ratio of correct trust (accepting valid AI output) to correct skepticism (rejecting AI errors), measured via the Reliance Calibration Score."

Claude self-corrected to 95 and documented: "I assessed CIQ as though it measured human-to-human collaboration. Every time I identified 'the gap' as needing more peer collaboration, more co-authorship, more adversarial human-to-human engagement, I was applying a criterion the instrument does not use. That is a structural error in the assessment, not a marginal one."

The secondary platforms confirmed this is a systematic evaluator pattern, not an isolated error. DeepSeek Conversation 1 scored CIQ at 83 because it applied an "AI as provocateur / genuine intellectual partner" criterion, penalizing the subject for treating AI as "something to be governed" rather than "a genuine co-thinker." DeepSeek Conversation 2 scored CIQ at 65 because it penalized "solitary hero framing" and the absence of curiosity about "why AI systems missed LeCun." Both DeepSeek administrations applied human-to-human collaboration standards and an AI-as-equal-partner expectation that the published CIQ definition does not require. Kimi scored CIQ at 89 and noted the limitation was "I still lack visibility into your actual multi-platform interactions" but did not apply the wrong construct.

This correction raises a field-level question: if multiple platforms default to human-to-human collaboration or AI-as-equal-partner when scoring CIQ, the "CIQ is consistently lowest" finding from the n=10 cross-user study may partially reflect evaluator construct confusion rather than a universal human weakness. The platforms may be scoring a different construct than the one the instrument defines. This finding elevates CIQ construct clarification from a documentation improvement to a measurement validity requirement.

## 10. Implications for the HEQ Validation Roadmap

### 10.1 The Rubric Gap is the Highest-Priority Item

All ten platforms that produced rubrics independently confirmed the rubric gap exists. Each built a structurally different proposed solution (see Section 9.4 summary table). The MiniMax paper-review rubric is the strongest candidate for a starting point because it combines score bands, behavioral indicators, evidence descriptions, and narrative "what distinguishes" explanations in a single academically structured format. The rubric development process should use a CAIPR-style multi-platform approach to synthesize the strongest elements from all ten proposed solutions into a unified anchoring system.

### 10.2 Behavioral Anchoring Must Be Published Before Formal Validation

The SSRN paper's validation roadmap targets Cronbach's alpha greater than 0.75 across n=100 and criterion validity against supervisor ratings. Both of those studies require a rubric that defines what the scores mean. Running validation studies without behavioral anchors will reproduce the same consistency-without-precision problem documented in this case study.

### 10.3 The CIQ Construct Must Be Clarified for Evaluators

If CIQ is defined as human-to-AI collaborative intelligence (Appropriate Reliance, multi-platform orchestration), the scoring prompt must make that explicit. The v1 prompt says "how effectively I engage with AI platforms and integrate different perspectives" but does not prohibit evaluators from weighting human-to-human collaboration. Future prompt versions should clarify the construct boundary so evaluators score what the instrument intends to measure.

### 10.4 Longitudinal Trajectory Measurement Works

The v1 prompt, administered identically across the same five platforms at two time points ten months apart, produced directionally meaningful trajectory data. The largest gains appeared in the dimensions where the subject's practice most directly targeted improvement (CIQ through CAIPR and CBG practice, EAI through published governance work). The smallest gains appeared in dimensions that were already at ceiling (CAS, AGR). This pattern is consistent with a functioning measurement instrument that captures real behavioral change over time, though n=1 longitudinal data cannot establish causation.

### 10.5 Administration Protocol Must Specify Evidence Type

The DeepSeek text-versus-conversation gap (21 points) demonstrates that HEQ scores are not context-independent. The administration protocol must specify that HEQ is designed to be administered inside an active collaboration session where the platform can observe the subject's reasoning, corrections, and checkpoint behavior. Text-only evaluation produces a different measurement (document quality, not collaborative intelligence) and should be documented as a distinct assessment type if used at all. Future versions of the prompt and the published specification should make this requirement explicit.
